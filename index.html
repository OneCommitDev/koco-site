Thesis

KoCo is not trying to be a generic startup studio.

The first version of KoCo should be simple: take small businesses with real revenue, real customers, and real operational friction, then improve the few systems that directly affect cash.

That means better websites, cleaner call handling, targeted prospecting, disciplined follow-up, tighter pricing, and lightweight automation that helps operators make decisions instead of creating more dashboards to ignore.

Approach

The KoCo playbook

KoCo should earn the right to own more by improving the basics first. The operating playbook is intentionally direct.

01

Find the revenue leak

Start with the places cash already wants to move: missed calls, stale websites, weak follow-up, unclear pricing, abandoned leads, or underused customer lists.

02

Fix the trust layer

Make the business credible where customers check first: website, search, listings, phone, catalog, quote flow, and the first email after contact.

03

Build the buyer list

Use narrow targeting instead of broad automation. For industrial products, define the real buyer, enrich contacts, and keep company-level exclusions clean.

04

Run a disciplined cadence

Calls, emails, callbacks, quotes, and follow-ups go into one scoreboard. Warm leads get same-day action. Dead ends get logged so the list improves.

05

Automate the repeatable work

Agents and software handle drafting, routing, research, reminders, and status reports. Humans keep judgment over pricing, promises, and strategic calls.

06

Repeat only after proof

KoCo should not add another company or product line until the current motion produces calls, quotes, orders, rentals, or a clear reason to stop.

Opportunities

What fits KoCo next

KoCo is best suited for businesses that are too practical for venture capital and too under-digitized to keep operating the old way.

Family and local businesses

Storage, services, distribution, light industrial, or specialty products with existing customers and a clear owner succession or modernization need.

Niche industrial products

Useful products with finite buyer lists, strong gross margins, and poor sales infrastructure. The smaller and more specific the buyer, the better.

China-to-US product opportunities

Specialty products where sourcing, language ability, and disciplined US distribution can create a small but durable operating business.

Software from lived problems

Products like OneCommit belong when KoCo has real domain insight, a reachable first user base, and a clean path to test retention and willingness to pay.
